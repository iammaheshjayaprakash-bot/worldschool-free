Ages 3–6

Explorers

The wonder years. Children at this stage are natural scientists — they touch, taste, ask, and imagine. Our curriculum channels that energy into structured discovery across 6 core areas.

Stage Overview — Explorers (Ages 3–6)

This stage builds the essential foundation for all future learning. Emphasis is on play-based discovery, language acquisition, number sense, and social-emotional security. All 6 subjects are delivered through stories, songs, movement, and hands-on activities.

📚

Language & Literacy

  • Phonemic awareness — sounds, rhymes, syllables
  • Letter recognition (upper & lowercase)
  • Sight words — first 100 high-frequency words
  • Listening comprehension & story recall
  • Oral storytelling & vocabulary building
  • Pre-writing skills — pencil grip, tracing, patterns
  • Introduction to reading simple sentences
🔢

Number & Early Mathematics

  • Counting 1–20 with one-to-one correspondence
  • Number recognition and writing 0–10
  • Shapes — 2D and basic 3D forms
  • Patterns — identifying and extending sequences
  • Measurement — size, weight, length comparisons
  • Sorting and classifying by attribute
  • Introduction to addition through objects
🌿

World Around Us

  • Living vs. non-living things
  • Plants — parts, growth, what they need
  • Animals — habitats, food, movement
  • Weather — sun, rain, wind, seasons
  • The five senses as scientific tools
  • My body — parts, health, hygiene
  • Community helpers and their roles
🎨

Arts & Imagination

  • Drawing — self-portraits, nature, free expression
  • Colour mixing — primary and secondary colours
  • Music — rhythm, pitch, singing, simple instruments
  • Drama — role play, puppetry, storytelling performance
  • Construction play — building with blocks and materials
  • Dance & movement to music
💛

Social-Emotional Learning

  • Naming and expressing emotions
  • Self-regulation — calming strategies
  • Empathy — understanding others' feelings
  • Friendship — sharing, taking turns, kindness
  • Problem-solving — conflict resolution basics
  • Resilience — trying again after difficulty
🏃

Movement & Physical Development

  • Gross motor — running, jumping, balancing, climbing
  • Fine motor — cutting, threading, clay, drawing
  • Body awareness and spatial understanding
  • Cooperative movement games
  • Healthy habits — sleep, food, hygiene
  • Outdoor exploration and nature play
1

The Sound Hunt — Phonics Introduction

LanguageAges 3–5Week 1

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the initial sounds of familiar words
  • Match spoken sounds to their letter symbols (s, a, t, p, i, n)
  • Develop phonemic awareness through rhyme and alliteration

Lesson Flow (45 min)

  • Warm-up (5 min): Clap-and-say syllable game with children's names
  • Story Time (10 min): Read "The Hungry Caterpillar" — emphasise initial sounds
  • Sound Hunt (15 min): Children walk the room finding objects starting with "s" and "t"
  • Letter Craft (10 min): Trace and decorate letter cards with matching drawings
  • Circle Reflection (5 min): Share one word that starts with today's sound

Materials Needed

Letter cards, small objects from classroom, crayons, "The Hungry Caterpillar" book (or similar)

2

Counting With Creatures — Numbers 1–10

MathematicsAges 3–6Week 1

Learning Objectives

  • Count objects 1–10 with one-to-one correspondence
  • Recognize written numerals 1–10
  • Understand "more" and "less" through comparison

Lesson Flow (40 min)

  • Opening Song (5 min): "Five Little Ducks" — count down together
  • Counting Mats (15 min): Place animal counters on numbered mats 1–10
  • Story Problem (10 min): "There are 4 butterflies. 2 more come. How many now?" (use objects)
  • Number Writing (10 min): Trace and write numerals 1–5 in sand trays

Materials Needed

Counting mats (printable), animal counters or beans, sand trays, number cards

3

Seeds & Sprouts — How Plants Grow

World Around UsAges 4–6Week 2

Learning Objectives

  • Name the basic parts of a plant (root, stem, leaf, flower)
  • Understand what plants need to grow (water, sun, soil)
  • Make and record a simple scientific observation

Lesson Flow (50 min)

  • Story (10 min): Read or tell "Jack and the Beanstalk" — discuss how the bean grew
  • Planting (20 min): Each child plants a bean seed in a clear cup to watch roots form
  • Plant Parts Drawing (15 min): Draw and label a plant with teacher support
  • Science Journal (5 min): Draw what they planted and predict what it will look like in 1 week

Materials Needed

Bean seeds, clear plastic cups, soil, water, drawing paper, pencils/crayons

4

Feelings are Okay — Emotional Vocabulary

SELAges 3–6Week 2

Learning Objectives

  • Name at least 6 core emotions (happy, sad, angry, scared, surprised, disgusted)
  • Connect emotions to facial expressions and body sensations
  • Practice one calming strategy independently

Lesson Flow (40 min)

  • Mirror Play (5 min): Make faces — can you show happy? Sad? Surprised?
  • Feelings Cards (10 min): Match emotion cards to story scenarios
  • Story (10 min): Read "The Colour Monster" — discuss why the monster is confused
  • Calm Corner Practice (10 min): Teach "belly breathing" — breathe in for 4, out for 4
  • Feelings Jar Craft (5 min): Children colour their own feelings jar

Materials Needed

Emotion face cards, "The Colour Monster" book, small mirrors, feelings jar printout, crayons

5

Colour Mixing Magic — Art & Science

ArtsScienceAges 3–6

Learning Objectives

  • Name the three primary colours
  • Predict and observe what happens when two colours mix
  • Express creativity through free painting

Lesson Flow (45 min)

  • Discussion (5 min): What colours do you know? What is your favourite?
  • Discovery Experiment (20 min): Use droppers and water with food colouring — mix red+yellow, blue+yellow, red+blue. Record results on prediction sheet.
  • Free Painting (20 min): Large paper, brushes, full palette — create anything you imagine

Materials Needed

Watercolour paints or food colouring, paper cups, droppers, large white paper, brushes, prediction worksheet

6

Weather Watchers — Seasons & Climate

ScienceAges 4–6

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the four seasons and their characteristics
  • Describe today's weather using correct vocabulary
  • Understand that weather affects what we wear and do

Lesson Flow (40 min)

  • Morning Meeting (5 min): Weather check — what do you see outside right now?
  • Seasons Wheel (15 min): Sort pictures into the four seasons wheel together
  • Dress the Character (10 min): Given a season, dress a paper character correctly
  • Weather Chart Setup (10 min): Start a class weather journal — record every day this week

Materials Needed

Season picture cards, weather chart poster, paper character with clothing cut-outs, glue, crayons

The Ecosystem Web — How Everything is Connected

ScienceAges 7–9

Learning Objectives

  • Define producer, consumer, and decomposer
  • Trace a food chain through 4+ links
  • Explain what happens if one link is removed

Lesson Flow (60 min)

  • Hook (5 min): "What would happen if all the bees disappeared?" Discussion.
  • Food Chain Yarn Web (20 min): Each student holds a "species" card. Use yarn to show connections. Then one "species" sits down — observe the cascade effect.
  • Diagram (15 min): Draw and label a food web with at least 6 organisms
  • Research Task (15 min): Find one endangered species and explain its role in an ecosystem
  • Discussion (5 min): Why does biodiversity matter?
2

Multiplication as Arrays — Visual Math

MathematicsAges 7–8

Learning Objectives

  • Represent multiplication with arrays
  • Understand the commutative property (3×4 = 4×3)
  • Apply multiplication to solve real-world problems

Lesson Flow (55 min)

  • Open Question (5 min): "I have 4 rows of chairs with 6 chairs in each row. How many chairs without counting one by one?"
  • Array Building (20 min): Use counters to build arrays. Record as multiplication sentences.
  • Grid Paper Drawing (15 min): Draw arrays for 3×7, 5×4, 6×8. Calculate area.
  • Word Problems (15 min): Solve 5 real-world multiplication stories
3

Writing to Persuade — Your First Opinion Essay

Language ArtsAges 8–10

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the structure of a persuasive essay (claim, evidence, conclusion)
  • Use at least 3 pieces of evidence to support an argument
  • Write a paragraph with a clear topic sentence

Lesson Flow (60 min)

  • Mentor Text (10 min): Read a model persuasive letter. Identify: What is the claim? What is the evidence?
  • Planning (15 min): Choose a topic (school should have longer recess / we should eat less meat / etc.). Fill in argument planner.
  • Draft (25 min): Write 3-paragraph essay using the planner
  • Peer Review (10 min): Exchange with a partner. Find: the claim, one piece of evidence, one thing to improve.

Ancient Egypt — Civilisation & Innovation

Social StudiesAges 8–10

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why the Nile River was central to Egyptian civilisation
  • Describe 3 major achievements of ancient Egypt
  • Connect ancient innovations to modern equivalents

Lesson Flow (60 min)

  • Map Work (10 min): Locate Egypt, the Nile, the Sahara. Why did civilisation grow along the river?
  • Innovation Gallery (20 min): Students rotate through 5 stations: pyramids, hieroglyphics, calendar, medicine, agriculture
  • Hieroglyphics Activity (15 min): Write their name in hieroglyphics using the ancient alphabet
  • Discussion (15 min): If you could bring one Egyptian invention to life today, what would it be and why?

Scratch Storytelling — First Code Project

The Double Helix — DNA, Genes & Inheritance

BiologyAges 12–14

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the structure of DNA and the role of genes
  • Explain dominant and recessive inheritance using Punnett squares
  • Discuss ethical implications of genetic technology

Lesson Flow (75 min)

  • Hook (5 min): "If you could know your future health risks, would you want to? Why or why not?"
  • DNA Structure (20 min): Model DNA using paper strips — base pairs, double helix, nucleotides
  • Punnett Square Practice (20 min): Eye colour, earlobe attachment — predict inheritance ratios
  • Case Study (20 min): CRISPR gene editing — read article, discuss benefits and risks
  • Ethics Debate (10 min): "Should parents be able to choose their child's traits?"
2

Solving Linear Equations — Algebra Foundations

MathematicsAges 11–13

Learning Objectives

  • Solve one and two-step linear equations
  • Use inverse operations to isolate a variable
  • Apply algebra to model and solve real-world problems

Lesson Flow (60 min)

  • Balance Model (10 min): Use a balance scale visual — whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other
  • Worked Examples (15 min): Solve 3x + 7 = 22, then 2(x-3) = 10 step-by-step with class
  • Independent Practice (20 min): 10 equations of increasing difficulty
  • Application (15 min): "A taxi costs $3 plus $2 per km. If the total was $17, how far did they travel?" Write and solve the equation.
3

The Causes of World War One — Historical Analysis

HistoryAges 12–14

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the four MAIN causes (Militarism, Alliance, Imperialism, Nationalism)
  • Analyse primary source documents from 1914
  • Construct a causal argument using evidence

Lesson Flow (75 min)

  • Timeline (10 min): Key events June–August 1914 — the assassination to the declaration
  • Source Analysis (25 min): Read 3 primary sources (newspaper excerpts, political cartoons, diplomatic telegrams)
  • MAIN Jigsaw (20 min): Groups become experts on one cause, teach the class
  • Essay Planning (20 min): Plan a response to: "Was the assassination of Franz Ferdinand the real cause of WWI?"
4

Python Fundamentals — Your First Real Program

ComputingAges 11–14

Learning Objectives

  • Write Python code using variables, input(), and print()
  • Use if/elif/else conditional logic
  • Build a complete functional program

Lesson Flow (75 min)

  • Live Coding (15 min): Build a name-greeter program together: name = input("What's your name? ") → print("Hello, " + name)
  • Add Conditions (20 min): Ask age, give different responses for under 13, 13–17, 18+
  • Personal Project (30 min): Build a quiz program on any topic of their choice (min. 3 questions)
  • Demo & Debug (10 min): Share with a partner — find and fix at least one bug together

Calculus — Derivatives and the Rate of Change

MathematicsAges 16–18

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the derivative as the instantaneous rate of change
  • Apply differentiation rules (power, product, chain)
  • Use derivatives to find maxima, minima, and optimise real problems

Lesson Flow (90 min)

  • Intuition (15 min): The speedometer in a car is a derivative. If distance is a function, speed is its derivative. Gradient of tangent line demonstration.
  • Rules Practice (25 min): Differentiate 12 functions using power rule, product rule, and chain rule
  • Optimisation Problem (30 min): "A farmer has 200m of fence. What dimensions give the maximum enclosed area?" — use derivatives to find the maximum.
  • Applications (20 min): Marginal cost in economics, velocity in physics, population growth in biology — derivatives everywhere.

Rawls & Justice — What is a Fair Society?

PhilosophyAges 16–18

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Rawls' "veil of ignorance" thought experiment
  • Compare Rawlsian justice with libertarian and utilitarian alternatives
  • Apply the framework to design a just educational system

Lesson Flow (80 min)

  • Thought Experiment (15 min): "Imagine you are about to be born. You don't know if you'll be rich or poor, healthy or disabled, in which country. What society would you design?"
  • Rawls' Two Principles (20 min): Read and discuss: the equal liberty principle and the difference principle
  • Comparison (20 min): How would Nozick (libertarian) and Bentham (utilitarian) disagree with Rawls?
  • Application (25 min): Design a "just" education system from behind the veil of ignorance. Present and defend it.
